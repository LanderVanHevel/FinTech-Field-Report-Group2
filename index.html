Before & After

How our thinking shifted:

A high-level overview

During the bootcamp, a few high-level beliefs changed most dramatically. Here's a quick overview, more detailed discussions on more specific topics follow in the timeline below.

What Fintech Actually Is
Before

Fintech is just a set of new tools, mostly powered by AI, that make existing financial services quicker, cheaper, and more digital.

↓
After

Finance is becoming part of larger digital systems, not just its own separate industry. Banking now happens quietly as part of everyday platforms, and banks are shifting from owning the customer relationship to acting as infrastructure providers.

Blockchain & Tokenisation
Before

Blockchain and tokenisation would remove the need for middlemen, replacing traditional financial institutions with fully decentralised systems.

↓
After

After the KBC tokenisation lab, we saw that banks and institutions remain important as they offer trust, compliance, and asset management. The future of finance will be a mix of new technology and existing institutions, not a purely decentralised one.

The Role of Regulation
Before

Regulation is something that gets in the way of fintech innovation. It is a constraint imposed from outside that slows good ideas down.

↓
After

Regulation actively shapes what is possible. Rules like PSD and new European frameworks set the pace and limits for innovation. The best companies don't fight regulation, they build within it, treating compliance as a design constraint rather than an obstacle.

Where the Hard Problems Are
Before

The hardest challenges in fintech are about getting the technology and strategy right. Build a good enough product with a clear plan and execution follows.

↓
After

The hardest problems are often organisational: people, structure, and motivation. Even with good ideas and solid plans, things fall apart if internal alignment is missing. This is exactly what we saw during the simulation game. Changing financial services is as much about culture as it is about technology.

01
Rabo Partnerships

Rabo Partnerships in Agriculture

✦ Personal Reflection
Roman
The session by Joris Timmers from Rabo Partnerships was honestly the highlight of the entire week for me. I didn’t know much about agricultural finance, and I had never heard of AgTech before. But his presentation made me see finance from a completely different, more global and human angle. What I loved most was learning how financial institutions can play a direct role in improving livelihoods, not just making profits.
✦ Personal Reflection
Charlotte
During this bootcamp, we spent a lot of time talking about high-tech solutions, but what truly hit home for me was the human side of FinTech. I used to think of FinTech as something for the 'tech-savvy' elite, but the session on Nickel and Rabo Partnerships completely flipped that script. Take Nickel, for example. They don't have fancy marble offices; they operate out of local newsagents and tobacco shops. I was stunned to learn that for 24% of their users, this was their first-ever bank account. In our team, we discussed how having a bank account is more than just a financial tool; it’s a gateway to dignity. Without it, you can’t even get a legal job.

A powerful realisation we had is that access to financial services can enable economic independence (especially for vulnerable groups) and acts as a tool against economic exclusion and inequality.

BeforeBanks are only here to make profit. If you can't provide any collateral on loans, they don't have a place for you. For example, the challenges of farmers: seasonality, volatility, perishability; make them “difficult clients” for banks, yet they’re essential for the world’s food system.

This exact combination of finance, sustainability, technology and global impact made us realise that FinTech isn’t only about cool apps, it’s also about creating real change.
02
Regulation

Regulation in FinTech

✦Personal Reflection
Lander
Is innovation being blocked by regulation? That is one of the main questions I had when tackling the subject of regulation for FinTechs. During our company visit to Simont Braun, I found it particularly interesting that financial regulation and the AI Act are deeply intertwined in the context of FinTech. As a result, FinTech companies face a dual regulatory burden, where they must comply with both financial regulations and AI-specific rules. This overlap can slow down the development and deployment of innovative solutions, as firms need to invest heavily in compliance before bringing products to market. However, I do believe we are going in the right direction of finetuning regulations to make to innovations as accessible as possible, even in Europe.

◈ Key Takeaway & Mindset Shift

FinTechs face a dual regulatory burden: financial regulations and AI specific regulations. During our discussion, we agreed regulations are necessary because applications in the financial sector are usually high risk. It therefore protects consumers from reckless innovation, while giving financial players a framework in order to innovate responsibly. This ensures FinTechs and banks keep trust with their customer base, what is extremely important in the financial sector.

BeforeRegulation mainly slows down innovation as it is constructed by non-technical people. While necessary, there is too much to take into account before banks/FinTechs can bring their technologically advanced financial products to the market.
→
AfterFinTech innovation cannot be separated from regulation. Regulation ensures trust, transparency and security for both the financial players and their customers, of the utmost importance in this sector. During the bootcamp, especially in the sessions with legal experts and startup founders, we saw that regulation isn't just something that gets in the way. Instead, it helps shape what is possible. Rules like PSD and new European regulations set the limits and speed for new ideas. This means there is always a balance between trying new things and following the rules, and the best companies manage to do both. No single actor can build the future of finance alone. Instead, value is created through collaboration between banks, fintech startups, BigTech companies, regulators, and other stakeholders. Each of these players controls a different part of the value chain, and understanding these interdependencies is crucial for any viable business model.
03
Digital currencies

Stable coins, smart contracts & tokenization

✦ Individual Reflection
Ingrid
The KBC Tokenisation Lab was the highlight of the bootcamp for me. It was where theory turned into practice. Before, I only knew blockchain as ideas like decentralisation and transparency. Working on a real example showed me how these ideas work in practice. Our group worked on tokenised real estate and noticed problems with the current system, like slow transactions, illiquidity, and high barriers to entry. Right now, you either own a whole property or join a big fund. There’s nothing in between. Tokenisation lets properties be split into digital shares, so people can own fractions, earn returns, and trade more easily. This process made me see blockchain differently. It doesn’t get rid of banks, but it changes what they do.
✦ Individual Reflection
Lander
If we look at the reason Bitcoin was created, are there real opportunities laying there for banks? Bitcoins' roots lie in the financial crisis of 2008, when its creator designed Bitcoin to address issues of trust and stability in traditional economic systems. The system provides a secure and anonymous transaction environment. This was my knowledge of crypto before starting the Fintech Bootcamp. However, we’ve seen banks and FinTechs use this technology in a different way. The question I feel we need to ask ourselves is: as crypto currencies all rely on the blockchain technology, which is designed as a sort of rebellion towards the heavily regulated financial sector, to what extent will such players be able to push products based on crypto assets to their customers, such as tokenization? To me, it feels this institutionalization of crypto defeats the whole purpose of why crypto and the blockchain were created in the first place.

◈ Key Takeaway & Mindset Shift

We started to see blockchain and tokenisation in a more balanced way. At first, it looked like these technologies would remove the need for middlemen. But after the KBC tokenisation lab, we saw that banks and similar institutions are still important, and in some ways, even more so. They offer trust, make sure rules are followed, and help manage assets. So, the future of finance will likely be a mix of new technology and existing institutions, not just fully decentralised systems.

Before Blockchain was created to avoid centralised control: crypto and tokenisation would remove the need for middlemen entirely. These technologies felt like a direct challenge to traditional financial institutions.
→
After Tokenisation lowers entry barriers, improves liquidity, and enables fractional ownership, making finance more accessible. But after the KBC tokenisation lab, we saw that banks remain essential for trust, compliance, and asset management. Stablecoins challenge systems like SWIFT by enabling faster and cheaper global transactions. Yet, we believe the future will likely be a mix of new technology and existing institutions, not full decentralisation. The tension between the original idea of crypto and how finance is being institutionalised is real, and unresolved.
02
Payments

The changing payment landscape: Wero

✦Personal Reflection
Sara
The integration of finance into everyday life will become more pronounced. As we saw with Wero, embedded finance is not just about adding payment solutions to apps like those of Uber or Shopify. It’s about creating an invisible infrastructure that allows financial services to seamlessly integrate into non-financial ecosystems. This means that finance will become a background service, and consumers will no longer need to consciously engage with financial products. Instead, these products will be seamlessly embedded into their daily digital interactions.
✦Personal Reflection
Roman
The session from Alfredo Prieto Rodrigo about Wero was one of my favourites - and very inspiring, especially as a European. Until now, I kind of accepted that VISA and Mastercard dominate almost all card payments. I never realised how strategically dependent Europe is on foreign payment infrastructure. The vision of Wero combining payments, loyalty, identity and even BNPL in one European ecosystem really impressed me. It’s one of the topics I’m the most excited to follow in the coming years.

◈ Key Takeaway & Mindset Shift

Our discussion on the opportunities and possibilities of Wero led to some interesting insights. We raised some concerns on how Visa and Mastercard would react to European banks supporting Wero. Are they going to tolerate this new player or will there be repercussions in the form of larger fees? As Wero is European and Visa/Mastercard remain global, banks will still have to issue cards that work on their networks for customers that travel internationally.

Before VISA and Mastercard dominate card payments, that's just the way it is. Europe's dependence on foreign payment infrastructure was never something we questioned or even noticed.
→
After Wero is Europe's attempt to reclaim digital payment sovereignty built on SEPA Instant, without relying on American card networks. The vision of combining payments, loyalty, identity and BNPL in one European ecosystem is compelling, and 65% of Belgians saying they'd use it makes it feel realistic. That said, the real challenge isn't the technology, it's behaviour. For most people, VISA or Mastercard isn't a conscious choice, it's just what sits behind their card. Getting people to actively switch is a problem Wero still needs to solve.
The Full Arc

How we see the future
of FinTech

After a week of deep dives, debates, and hands-on projects, here is how we think the future of FinTech will look like.

Finance becomes invisible infrastructure

Financial services are no longer something you go out of your way to use. They are quietly embedded into the platforms and experiences people already use every day, from shopping apps to your mobile banking app serving as a super app. It is just a matter of time before more and more people become used to this idea.

Banks pivot into the role of trust providers

Banks won't disappear, but their role is shifting a little. Rather than owning the full customer relationship at all times, they are becoming the trusted backbone. Just think of the new technologies that are available due to Open Banking and BaaS. Banks handle compliance, custody, and regulation while FinTechs and platforms own the user experience.

Blockchain and tokenisation reshape ownership

Tokenisation will make it possible to own fractions of assets that were previously inaccessible: real estate, funds, even agricultural insurance. This lowers barriers to entry and could fundamentally democratise investing and financial protection.

Regulation shapes what is possible, not just what is allowed

The future of fintech will not be built around regulation, it will be built through it. Rules like PSD and the EU AI Act are not just guardrails, but are used by innovating companies as frameworks to work in. The companies that thrive will be those that treat compliance as a design constraint.

Technological advancements are easy, change management will be the bottleneck

The biggest barrier to FinTech's future is not technology or strategy, it is execution, alignment, and organisational change. Good ideas fail without the right people, structure, and coordination behind them. The future belongs to those who can manage both innovation and implementation.

FinTech's real measure is social impact

The future of finance should not only be measured by ROI, but by the number of lives it helps transform. Whether that means a first bank account for someone previously excluded (Nickel), micro-insurance for a smallholder farmer (Rabobank & Nagro), or financial autonomy for a vulnerable person. We believe the most meaningful FinTechs are the kind that reach those who need it most.
